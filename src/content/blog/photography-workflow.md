People sometimes ask how I edit my photos. The honest answer is: slowly. A single landscape image can take me anywhere from 20 minutes to an hour, depending on how much I'm fighting the light.

## In the Field

I shoot exclusively in RAW on my Nikon Z5II. RAW files are larger and need processing, but they give you so much more flexibility in post. Highlights that look blown out? Usually recoverable. Shadow detail? It's there, you just have to pull it out.

I bracket exposures for high-contrast scenes — typically three shots at -2, 0, and +2 EV. Not for HDR (I find most HDR looks overdone), but so I have options when editing.

## Import and Cull

Everything goes into Lightroom Classic, organized by date and location. I do a quick pass to flag the keepers — usually about 20% of what I shot. The rest gets rejected. Being ruthless here saves time later.

## The Edit

My editing style is minimal. I'm going for a slightly cool, desaturated look — not the oversaturated Instagram landscape style.

Typical adjustments:
- **White balance** — usually cooler than what the camera chose
- **Exposure and contrast** — subtle, maybe ±0.5 stops
- **Highlights down, shadows up** — but not aggressively
- **Clarity** — a touch, around +10-15
- **HSL adjustments** — desaturate greens slightly, shift blues toward teal
- **Sharpening** — for web export, around 40-60 with masking

## Export

For the web: JPEG at 2400px long edge, quality 85. For prints: full resolution TIFF. I keep the Lightroom catalog as my single source of truth — no Photoshop unless I need to remove something specific.

## The Takeaway

The best editing is invisible. If someone looks at your photo and thinks "nice edit," you've probably gone too far. The goal is for them to think "nice photo."
