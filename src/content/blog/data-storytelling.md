There's a moment in every client presentation where the room either leans in or checks out. It usually has nothing to do with how sophisticated your analysis is. It's about whether you told a story.

## The Problem with Dashboards

Most dashboards are graveyards of good analysis. Dozens of charts, filters everywhere, and no clear takeaway. I've built plenty of these myself early on. The data was right, but nobody acted on it.

## What Changed

At PwC, I started framing every analysis as a narrative: here's what happened, here's why it matters, here's what to do about it. Three acts. Simple structure. The same data suddenly became actionable.

## The Framework

1. **Context** — What's the situation? What does the audience already know?
2. **Conflict** — What's surprising, broken, or changing in the data?
3. **Resolution** — What should we do about it?

It sounds basic, but most analysts skip straight to the resolution without building the case. The conflict is what makes people care.

## What I'm Still Learning

Visual design. A well-designed chart does half the storytelling work. I've been studying Edward Tufte's principles and trying to apply them to my Power BI reports. Less ink, more insight.
